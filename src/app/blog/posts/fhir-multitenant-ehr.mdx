When you promise **global interoperability**, FHIR is the right default — but tenants, workflows, and national profiles do not line up with a clean textbook model. This is how I think about shipping FHIR inside a **multi-tenant EHR** without painting the team into a corner.

## Problem

You need external systems to consume clinical data through **standard resources and APIs**, while each hospital still expects custom forms, local coding systems, and strict isolation from other tenants. If you model everything as “just FHIR,” you leak tenant concerns into shared layers; if you hide FHIR entirely, you rebuild integrations by hand forever.

## Approach

- **Separate “clinical truth” from “exchange shape.”** Persist what the hospital operates on; map to FHIR (R4 or your national baseline) at boundedaries: outbound APIs, bulk export, and partner onboarding — not necessarily in every internal read path.
- **Version and profile explicitly.** Lock a **FHIR version** and document which **profiles** you support. “We support FHIR” is not a spec; “Patient, Encounter, Observation per X IG vY” is.
- **Tenant isolation first.** Authorization must resolve tenant → organization → user before any resource ID is meaningful. FHIR’s URLs are not a substitute for authZ.
- **Start with a small, high-value surface.** Patient demographics, encounters, and a slice of observations often unlock the first integrations; expand resource types when you have a consumer contract, not when someone reads the full spec.

## Lessons

- **Custom extensions** will appear — budget time for governance (who can define them, how they map to partner systems).
- **Performance** matters on hot lists (active patients, today’s appointments). Not every query should be a naive FHIR search against normalized tables.
- **Testing** against reference servers or partner sandboxes early avoids “it validates in Postman but fails in production.”

FHIR is not a feature flag — it is a **contract**. Treat it like one and your EHR can grow with new hospitals without rewriting the core for every integration.
